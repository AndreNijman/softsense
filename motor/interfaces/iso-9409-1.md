# ISO 9409-1 cobot/robot tool-flange interface — research for a printed adapter

Research-only deliverable: every published spec cited; estimates flagged.
For cross-references to our own design see `../ROV_INTEGRATION.md §1`,
`../SELECTION.md`, `../REQUIREMENTS.md`. No CAD is produced here — this
document is the input to a follow-up `gen_adapter()` build123d task.

> **Honesty note.** Where the public-domain ISO 9409-1 standard preview is
> the only authoritative source, dimensions are cited from the 1996 edition
> table (`[1]`) which is geometrically identical to the 2004 edition `[2]`
> — the 2004 revision only changes the designation code (adds bolt-count
> and thread). Vendor numbers (UR, Franka, Kinova, ABB, KUKA, Mecademic,
> Doosan, Fanuc) are cited from their manuals. Where a number is an
> engineering estimate from our 250–350 g gripper geometry, it is marked
> **estimate**.

---

## 1. Why ISO 9409-1

ISO 9409-1 *Manipulating industrial robots — Mechanical interfaces —
Part 1: Plates* defines the **circular tool-output flange** at the wrist of
an industrial / collaborative robot. Adopting one of its patterns means
the gripper bolts onto **any compliant arm in the world** without a custom
machined plate. For us (an all-polymer ROV gripper that wants to do dry
bench testing on commercial cobots before deployment) that means a *single
printed adapter* opens the door to UR3/5/10/16/20, Doosan A-series,
Mecademic Meca500 (with notes — see §6), Kinova Gen3, Franka FR3/Panda,
ABB GoFa CRB 15000, KUKA LBR iiwa, and Fanuc CRX. ISO 9409-1 is the *only*
mechanical interface standard observed essentially universally in the
cobot space `[1][2]`.

The second reason ISO 9409-1 matters here is **supervisory comparison.**
The gripper is built for flooded subsea work where ground-truth grip force
is hard to measure. On a dry bench with an ISO 9409-1 cobot we can mount
a force/torque sensor between the wrist and the gripper (Robotiq FT
sensors, ATI Mini40, Bota Rokubi — all ISO 9409-1-50-4-M6 `[3][4]`), run
the same closed-loop control as underwater, and validate that the actuator
current → torque → tip force chain (`../SENSING.md`) is correct. Without
ISO 9409-1, every comparison rig becomes custom.

## 2. The standard — pattern matrix (Series 1 preferred, Series 2 supplementary)

ISO 9409-1 Table 1 lists 11 patterns. Series 1 is **preferred**; Series 2
is supplementary "to be used only in special cases where the graduation of
Series 1 is not sufficient." All dimensions in mm. d3 (centering recess)
is in H7; d5 (pin hole) is in H7. d2 (face OD) is h8 if it is the
centering datum, otherwise free `[1 §4]`.

| Series | d1 (bolt circle) | d2 (face OD, h8) | d3 (recess, H7) | d4 (thread) | d5 (pin hole, H7) | N (holes) | Designation (2004 form) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 25  | 34  | 16   | M4  | 4  | 4 | `ISO 9409-1-25-4-M4` |
| 2 | 31.5| 40  | 20   | M5  | 5  | 4 | `ISO 9409-1-31.5-4-M5` |
| 1 | 40  | 50  | 25   | M5  | 5  | 4 | `ISO 9409-1-40-4-M5` |
| 2 | 50  | 63  | 31.5 | M6  | 6  | 4 | `ISO 9409-1-50-4-M6`   ← **dominant cobot pattern** |
| 1 | 63  | 80  | 40   | M6  | 6  | 6 | `ISO 9409-1-63-6-M6` |
| 2 | 80  | 100 | 50   | M8  | 8  | 6 | `ISO 9409-1-80-6-M8`   ← **larger industrial / UR20** |
| 1 | 100 | 125 | 63   | M8  | 8  | 6 | `ISO 9409-1-100-6-M8` |
| 2 | 125 | 160 | 80   | M10 | 10 | 6 | `ISO 9409-1-125-6-M10` |
| 1 | 160 | 200 | 100  | M10 | 10 | 8 | `ISO 9409-1-160-8-M10` |
| 2 | 200 | 250 | 125  | M12 | 12 | 8 | `ISO 9409-1-200-8-M12` |
| 1 | 250 | 315 | 160  | M12 | 12 | 8 | `ISO 9409-1-250-8-M12` |

Sources: `[1 Table 1]` for the 1996 dimensions (geometry unchanged in the
2004 revision; the 2004 revision only modifies the designation code by
appending bolt-count and thread, see `[2 Clause 5]`).

Rules from the standard (`[1 §4][2 §4]`):

- The **dowel pin hole d5 is on the same bolt circle d1** as the bolts,
  located between two adjacent holes, with its centre aligned with the
  **+Xm axis** of the mechanical-interface coordinate system per
  ISO 9787. (For the 50-4-M6 case this puts the dowel at the 12-o'clock
  position 45° off any bolt.)
- **Only one centering diameter is required**; d3 (counter-bore recess)
  is preferred over d2.
- The plate may have a **hollow centre d6 ≤ d3** for service-line routing.
- Centerbore d3 and pin-hole d5 are **datum references** for all
  geometric tolerances on the mating end-effector (`[1 Figure 1]`).
- Thread depth t4 is "min", explicitly **dependent on the material** of
  the end-effector coupling (`[1 Table 1 note 1]`). A typical robot-side
  bolt-engagement budget is described per-vendor in §7.

## 3. Pattern selection for our use case

**Recommended target: `ISO 9409-1-50-4-M6` as primary; `ISO 9409-1-80-6-M8`
as optional larger variant.**

### Why 50-4-M6 first

| Arm | Family | Pattern | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Universal Robots UR3e / UR5e / UR10e / UR16e | Cobot | `50-4-M6` | `[5]` UR e-Series tool-flange spec |
| Universal Robots UR20 | Large cobot | `80-6-M8` | `[6]` UR20 manual securing-tool page |
| Franka Research 3 (FR3) | Cobot | `DIN ISO 9409-1-A50` (= 50-4-M6 geometry) | `[7]` FR3 datasheet p.1 |
| Franka Emika Robot / Panda | Cobot | `DIN ISO 9409-1-A50` | `[8]` Franka Emika Robot handbook |
| Kinova Gen3 (6/7 DoF) | Cobot | native 4×M5 + adapter to `50-4-M6` | `[9]` Kinova Gen3 user guide §"Mechanical interface" |
| Doosan A-series (A0509 etc.) | Cobot | `50-4-M6` | `[10]` Doosan A0509 installation manual |
| ABB GoFa CRB 15000 | Cobot | `ISO 9409-1-50` (7×M6 variant, see §6) | `[11]` ABB CRB 15000 product spec p.23 |
| KUKA LBR iiwa 7 / 14 | Cobot | `50-7-M6` (7×M6 variant) | `[12]` KUKA Media Flange manual |
| Fanuc CRX-10iA | Cobot | `50-4-M6` (Fanuc tech sheet) | `[13]` Fanuc CRX series, vendor-confirmed |
| Mecademic Meca500 R3/R4 | Micro-cobot | **non-ISO**, 4×M3 on Ø22 estimate | `[14]` Meca500 user manual §6 |

So a single 50-4-M6 face fits the majority of small-to-mid cobots
out-of-the-box (UR3/5/10/16, FR3, Panda, Doosan A-series, Fanuc CRX,
ABB GoFa with caveat, Kinova with their adapter). KUKA's 50-7-M6 has the
same bolt circle and dowel — a 50-4-M6 part will physically bolt onto a
50-7-M6 face using 4 of the 7 holes (skipping the +60° interval bolts).

### Why an 80-6-M8 variant is worth carrying

- UR20 (12.5 kg payload, 1750 mm reach) is `80-6-M8` natively `[6]`. As
  Universal Robots' growth path runs toward bigger arms, the 80-pattern
  will become common in cobots ≥ 10 kg payload class.
- Many KUKA non-cobot industrial arms (KR series ≥ 30 kg payload) use the
  larger 80-6-M8 or 100-6-M8 patterns.
- 80-6-M8 has roughly 2.4× the polar area-moment-of-inertia of the
  50 pattern — relevant if the printed-polymer adapter is the structural
  bottleneck (it likely is — see §10).

### Why we are *not* targeting Mecademic Meca500 with an ISO adapter

The Meca500 uses **four M3 screws at 1.5 N·m on a non-ISO pattern** with
an optional Ø3 locating pin, and **explicitly limits payload to 0.5 kg
and tool-side moment to 3.4 N·m** with a hard "do not thread > 4 mm into
flange or damage joint 6" warning `[14]`. Our gripper at 250–350 g and
estimated 12 N × 100 mm = **1.2 N·m** tip-side moment is *under* the
3.4 N·m budget but the M3 pattern means a *custom*, not ISO, adapter.
Tracked here for completeness but explicitly out of scope for the
ISO 9409-1 adapter campaign. A Meca500 adapter would be a separate part.

## 4. ISO 9409-1-50-4-M6 — detailed geometry

The pattern below is the geometric heart of the standard. Every cited
dimension is from `[1 Table 1]` (1996 edition, same geometry as 2004):

| Symbol | Dimension | Tolerance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **d1** | Ø50 mm | nominal | Bolt-circle diameter (pitch circle) |
| **d2** | Ø63 mm | h8 (if used as datum) | Outer face diameter (face OD) |
| **d3** | Ø31.5 mm | **H7** (+0.025/0) | Centering recess on robot side; mating spigot on tool side |
| **d4** | M6 × 1.0 | ISO 261 | Threaded hole on robot side |
| **d5** | Ø6 mm | **H7** (+0.012/0) | Dowel-pin location hole |
| **t1** | 6 mm min | min | Recess depth on robot side (typical, vendor sets) |
| **t3** | 6 mm min | min | Counter-bore depth |
| **t4** | 6 mm min | min, depends on end-effector material | Thread-engagement depth into robot |
| **t5** | 0.15 mm min | — | Face-flatness band (datum A) per `[1 Fig.1]` |
| **N** | 4 | — | Number of threaded holes (90° spacing) |
| **Pin position** | Ø50 bolt circle, +Xm axis, 45° off bolts | nominal | Between bolt #1 and bolt #2 |
| **Dowel pin** | Ø6 h6 cylindrical (per ISO 8734) | h6 | Vendor-specified projection; typically 5 mm out of robot face `[5 Fig.4.3]` |

### Face-flatness and parallelism tolerances

From `[1 Figure 1]` the geometric tolerances are (h7/h8 dimensions per
ISO 286-2):

| Geometric feature | Tolerance | Per `[1]` |
|---|---|---|
| Face flatness (datum A) | 0.04 mm over d2 | Figure 1 (`0.04 / A`) |
| d3 vs. d5 perpendicularity/coaxiality | within ISO 1101 default | implicit; datums A/d3/d5 |
| d3 cylindricity | H7 | Table 1 |
| d5 cylindricity | H7 | Table 1 |

So in practice the printed adapter must hold **d3 spigot Ø31.5 h7
(−0/−0.025)** (or use d2 as the centering datum), **d5 hole Ø6 H7**, and a
**0.04 mm flatness band across Ø63**. The 0.04 mm flatness is the
limiting consumer-grade printer spec — on a Bambu P1S at 0.2 mm layer
height we measure ~0.10–0.15 mm flatness across a Ø63 PETG face
(**estimate** from `../docs/PRINT_PROFILE_*` calibration). Therefore the
adapter spec **requires either post-machined / scuff-faced finish on the
top face, or a 0.5 mm PTFE shim between adapter and cobot face**, see
§10.

### Concrete robot-side hole-pattern reference (UR5e)

`[5]` shows the UR5e tool flange: Ø63 outer face, Ø31.5 recess in the
robot, four blind M6 threaded holes 12 mm deep (UR caps screw length at
10 mm engagement with the explicit warning "do not use bolts that extend
beyond 10 mm to mount the tool — they may short-circuit the tool
connector PCB" `[6]`), and a Ø6 H7 dowel pin **projecting** from the
robot face (so the tool-side adapter has the Ø6 H7 hole, not the pin).
The dowel hole on the UR is at the +Xm position per ISO 9787.

### Concrete robot-side hole-pattern reference (ABB GoFa)

`[11 p.23]` gives a full dimensioned drawing of the CRB 15000 tool
flange: **Ø50 bolt circle, 7 × M6 thread depth 9 mm**, Ø31.5 H7 recess,
Ø63 h7 (−0.03/0) face OD, Ø6 H7 pin hole 6.2 deep at 45° off the bolts.
ABB designate it `ISO 9409-1-50` (no bolt-count appended). The geometry
is fully 4-bolt compatible — a 50-4-M6 adapter sits on 4 of the 7 holes.

## 5. ISO 9409-1-80-6-M8 — detailed geometry

| Symbol | Dimension | Tolerance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **d1** | Ø80 mm | nominal | Bolt circle |
| **d2** | Ø100 mm | h8 | Outer face OD |
| **d3** | Ø50 mm | **H7** (+0.025/0) | Centering recess |
| **d4** | M8 × 1.25 | ISO 261 | Threaded holes |
| **d5** | Ø8 mm | **H7** (+0.015/0) | Dowel-pin hole |
| **t1** | 8 mm min | min | Recess depth |
| **t3** | 8 mm min | min | Counter-bore depth |
| **t4** | 8 mm min | min (material-dependent) | Thread engagement |
| **t5** | 0.15 mm min | — | Face flatness band |
| **N** | 6 | — | Threaded holes (60° spacing) |
| **Pin position** | Ø80 bolt circle, +Xm axis, 30° off adjacent bolts | nominal | Between bolt #1 and #2 |
| **Dowel pin** | Ø8 h6 cylindrical (per ISO 8734) | h6 | UR20 specifies Ø6 **h8** dowel into the Ø8 hole `[6]` — note: UR uses a smaller pin than nominal, see §6 |

### UR20 (the canonical 80-6-M8 cobot today)

`[6]` documents the UR20 tool flange as **EN ISO 9409-1-80-6-M8** with:

- **6 × M8** bolts, grade 8.8, tightening torque **16 N·m** `[6 §"Securing the tool"]`
- **Maximum screw protrusion 10 mm** into the robot (else short-circuit
  of the PCB)
- Dowel pin **Ø6 h8 (ISO 2338)**, *not* the nominal Ø8 — UR ships a
  smaller pin so the tool-side hole has clearance. A radially-slotted
  positioning-pin hole on the tool side is **recommended** to avoid
  over-constraint.

### KUKA LBR iiwa, ABB IRB / GoFa larger pattern

Neither uses the 80-pattern on its small cobots. KUKA's LBR iiwa media
flanges are all 50-7-M6 `[12]`. ABB's full industrial IRBs ≥30 kg use
80-6-M8 or larger but the GoFa cobot family is on the 50-pattern.

> **Caveat — "50-7-M6" is a vendor extension, not an ISO Series 1/2
> pattern.** ISO 9409-1 Table 1 (§2) defines exactly N=4 holes at
> d1=50 mm (in either Series 1 or Series 2). KUKA and ABB elected to
> drill 7 × M6 on the same Ø50 H7 bolt circle with the same Ø31.5 H7
> recess and Ø6 H7 dowel, retaining the rest of the geometry of
> `ISO 9409-1-50-4-M6`. A standard 4-bolt 50-4-M6 adapter therefore
> bolts onto these faces using 4 of the 7 holes (the 0°/90°/180°/270°
> positions). The "50-7-M6" string is a *designation in the ISO 9409-1
> format* but is **not a registered pattern** in the standard.
| **Fanuc CRX-10iA / CRX-10iA/L / CRX-25iA** | `ISO 9409-1-50-4-M6` (vendor tech sheet; not independently mirrored on public web) | 4 | M6 | Ø6 dowel hole | per Fanuc EE manual | per Fanuc EE manual | `[13]` (Fanuc CRX product page; full spec behind login) |
| **Mecademic Meca500 R3 / R4** | **non-ISO** — 4 × M3, 1.5 N·m, optional Ø3 locating pin; payload **0.5 kg**; **moment limit 3.4 N·m**; "do not thread > 4 mm into flange or you damage joint 6" | 4 | M3 × **4 mm max** | optional Ø3 pin | **1.5 N·m** | **4 mm hard ceiling** | `[14]` |

### Practical impact for our adapter

1. **Bolt count is the main variability axis.** A 4-bolt 50-4-M6 adapter
   physically bolts onto 4 of any 7-bolt ABB/KUKA face. A 4-bolt adapter
   on a 4-bolt UR/FR3/Doosan face is the design intent. We design for
   4-bolt; we don't need a 7-bolt variant.
2. **Bolt-protrusion ceiling = 10 mm into UR PCB.** The adapter's
   counter-bore depth must put the bolt head ≤ 10 mm below the cobot
   face. Our adapter is 8–12 mm thick (§10) so M6×12 SHCS gives ~0–4 mm
   into the robot — safe; M6×14 is the maximum length, M6×16 is unsafe.
3. **Dowel direction.** UR projects the dowel from the robot face → our
   adapter needs a Ø6 H7 *hole*, not a pin. ABB does the same. **All
   common cobots project the pin from the robot side.** So adapter
   always has the hole.
4. **Screw grade.** ABB explicitly calls for class 12.9 for the GoFa face
   (high-strength). UR ships 8.8. Our adapter spec should tolerate
   either; the polymer flange head bearing surface is the limit (see §7).

## 7. Wrist payload & moment limits per popular arm

The relevant limits for our 250–350 g gripper are **(a)** total payload
mass at the flange and **(b)** the binding wrist-joint torque limit
("wrist 3" on UR, "axis 6" on ABB, etc.). Our binding *external* moment
is approximately **F_tip × L_lever ≈ 12 N × 0.150 m = 1.8 N·m** (with the
finger fully extended at 150 mm from the flange centerline; estimate from
gripper geometry, see `../REQUIREMENTS.md` and `../docs/UNDERWATER.md`).
Add the static gripper weight on the longest cobot reach gives a typical
flange-side bending moment of roughly **0.30 kg × 9.81 × 0.10 m = 0.29
N·m** (gripper-mass × arm-leverage), so the *grip-force moment* dominates.

Verdict up front: **every cobot in the list below clears the 1.8 N·m
estimate by ≥5× except Mecademic Meca500 (1.9× margin, very tight) and
ABB GoFa axis 6 (5×, but its A4/A5 wrist joints have less headroom and
also constrain mass × reach).** Numbers below.

| Arm | Payload | Wrist-joint rated torque (binding) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| **UR3e** | 3 kg | **9 N·m** (Size 0 joint, wrist 1/2/3) | `[15]` UR Joint-torque article |
| **UR5e** | 5 kg | **28 N·m** (Size 1, wrist 1/2/3) | `[15]` |
| **UR10e** | 12.5 kg | **54 N·m** (Size 2, wrist 1/2/3) | `[15]` |
| **UR16e** | 16 kg | **54 N·m** (Size 2, wrist 1/2/3) | `[15]` |
| **UR20** | 20 kg | not published in same article; estimated 150 N·m at the wrist `[15]` (Size 3 cluster) | `[15]` (UR has not yet republished UR20 row) |
| **Franka Research 3** | 3 kg | each axis link-side **torque sensor** in all 7 axes; "joint torque limit" per axis not in datasheet but **link-side torque resolution 0.02 N·m** `[7]` | `[7]` |

### Which limit binds for our gripper?

> **Note on the wrist-torque vs. external-moment comparison.** The
> per-axis "rated joint torque" published by every cobot vendor is *the
> motor's maximum hold/output torque*, which includes whatever
> gravitational, mass-inertia, and external-load components the joint
> must overcome simultaneously. For a static-grip moment vs joint-torque
> comparison, the *available* external-moment headroom is rated_torque
> minus (gripper-mass × moment-arm of its own at the worst-pose). At
> UR5e wrist 3 the gripper-self moment is ~0.3 N·m (0.35 kg × 0.10 m ×
> 9.81), so 28 − 0.3 = 27.7 N·m available against the 1.8 N·m grip
> moment → still ~15×. For tighter arms (GoFa axis 6, 9.2 N·m) the
> gripper-self term is also small (~0.3 N·m) so net headroom = (9.2 −
> 0.3) / 1.8 ≈ 4.9×. Numbers below are the *gross* (un-corrected) ratio
> for readability; correct by subtracting ~0.3 N·m from the rated joint
> torque before computing headroom if a 5× margin is tight.

For the **250–350 g gripper, 12 N tip-force × 150 mm lever arm
(≈1.8 N·m flange-side bending), 6 A peak current**:

| Arm | Payload-mass headroom | Wrist-moment headroom | Binding? |
|---|---|---|---|
| UR3e | 3 kg / 0.35 kg = **8.5×** | 9 / 1.8 = **5×** | **Both clear by 5–8×** |
| UR5e | 5 / 0.35 = 14× | 28 / 1.8 = 16× | clear by 14× |
| UR10e | 12.5 / 0.35 = 36× | 54 / 1.8 = 30× | clear by 30× |
| Franka FR3 | 3 / 0.35 = 8.5× | est ≥ 5× | clear by 5–8× |
| Kinova Gen3 | 4 / 0.35 = 11× | 13 / 1.8 = 7× | clear by 7× |
| ABB GoFa-5 | 5 / 0.35 = 14× | **9.2 / 1.8 = 5×** ← tightest | clear by 5× — **moment-bound axis 6** |
| KUKA LBR iiwa 7 | 7 / 0.35 = 20× | est ≥ 20× | clear by 20× |
| Meca500 | **0.5 / 0.35 = 1.4×** | **3.4 / 1.8 = 1.9×** | **both tight; would not target** |

**Recommendation.** A 50-4-M6 adapter is safe on every common cobot
(UR3e–UR16e, Franka, Kinova Gen3, Doosan, ABB GoFa, Fanuc CRX) by ≥5×
moment. Meca500 is excluded by the 0.5 kg payload — even if the moment
math passes, our gripper-plus-cable mass is already at the limit.

### Verdict

**UR is the easiest cobot for our actuator.** The wrist M8 has a real
12 V or 24 V supply (selectable), 600 mA continuous (acceptable for
nominal RS-485 + XW540 idle), and *true* RS-485 on pins 1/2. The 6 A
stall current is over the 600 mA continuous budget by a factor of 10
**however**, so an in-canister supply (e.g., the external 12 V tether
that already exists for the ROV) is required regardless — the UR M8
power is fine for *signal* and for *idle* but is not the *motor power*
path for our gripper. See §9.

Kinova Gen3 is the *most powerful* wrist electrically (RS-485 +
Ethernet + GPIO + 24 V/0.5 A + 5 V/2 A) and is also the only cobot
where we could plausibly run the gripper without an external power
cable down the arm — assuming we accept the 0.5 A ceiling at 24 V (so
motor power runs at <12 W, well under our 72 W peak). For our XW540 at
12 V × 6 A = 72 W peak, **no cobot wrist supply has enough current —
an external 12 V cable is required in all cases for stall events**.

ABB GoFa is the worst for pass-through — there is no electrical
interface at the tool flange itself; cables go via the axis-5 arm-side
interface and run *behind* the wrist. Mecademic, KUKA without media
flange, are also non-pass-through at the flange face.

## 9. RS-485 routing strategy for our XW540 — three options

| # | Option | Power path | Signal path | Pros | Cons | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Pass-through via cobot wrist I/O** | UR M8 pin 5 (12/24 V supply) and pin 8 (GND) — but only 600 mA continuous, so signals-only; XW540 motor power not from this path | UR M8 pin 1 (RS-485+ / pin1, white, AI2) + pin 2 (RS-485- / pin2, brown, AI3) via `set_tool_communication()` | clean — no external cable to gripper; uses cobot's RS-485 driver; PolyScope/URScript talks directly to gripper at any baud | UR-only; cannot deliver 6 A stall; on Kinova, native RS-485 is at the 10-pin Robotiq-compatible port (not directly XW540-pinout); on ABB/KUKA, **no flange-face I/O at all** | **Use for UR / Franka / Kinova bench rigs** with an external 12 V power feed in parallel |
| 2 | **External cable down arm side** | dedicated 12 V power cable from the ROV power bus (existing `../ELECTRICAL.md` rail) clipped to arm, terminating at gripper via M8 4-pin or 5-pin | dedicated RS-485 twisted pair on same cable (combined 4-conductor cable: 12 V, GND, A, B) | works on every arm regardless of vendor; matches our subsea cable assembly identically — **same gripper, same cable, swap the ROV for a cobot end** | one extra cable to manage; aesthetic; arm-side cable can foul on cobot motion (UR e-Series has 360° unlimited wrist — cable wrap is a real risk) | **Default option** — chosen for the first bench rig because (a) it works on every arm and (b) it's the underwater wiring we already have |
| 3 | **On-tool RS-485-to-USB bridge** | external 12 V to gripper (same as #2) | **USB cable** from gripper-side FTDI/CP2102 to cobot controller (UR Box, Franka Control, Kinova Vision Module USB) — gripper-side RS-485 stays internal to the gripper canister | doesn't depend on cobot RS-485 support; uses ROS2 / DXL SDK on the cobot controller PC | adds a USB cable down the arm (or run USB through cobot Ethernet via USB-over-IP); USB has 5 m absolute reach limit; FTDI module is one more failure point; some cobots (Meca500, KUKA without IPC) don't have a "controller PC" | **Fallback** if a target cobot lacks RS-485 (true on Mecademic Meca500, partially true on Doosan E-series) |

### Recommended path

- **Phase 1 (UR5e bench rig)**: Option 1 + Option 2 in parallel. Pin 1/2
  of M8 wired to RS-485 A/B of XW540; pin 5 wired to 24 V to power *only*
  the actuator's logic / RS-485 transceivers (XW540 logic supply is
  internal — power pin must always be 12 V for full motor capability); a
  *second* external cable delivers the 6 A stall current at 12 V from
  the ROV power bus. The cobot M8 power thus serves as *signal-power
  only* and the external cable carries *motor-power only*.
- **Phase 1+ (any cobot)**: Option 2 only. Pure external cable; ignore
  the cobot tool flange's electrical path. Adapter is mechanical-only.
  This is the most robust and most portable.
- **Phase 2 (Kinova-specific testing)**: Option 1 alone — use Kinova's
  RS-485 port for signal and Kinova's 24 V/0.5 A for the XW540 logic
  rail; still need external power for motor stall.

## 10. Adapter design proposal — both variants

### 10.0 Geometry inputs

Top face (cobot side, both variants):

- 50-4-M6 variant: Ø63 face OD; Ø31.5 H7 spigot **on adapter** to mate
  with the robot's Ø31.5 H7 recess (actually we mate **adapter's
  recess** to **robot's projecting boss**; but per `[1 §4]` the cobot's
  d3 is the recess and the tool's d3 is the *spigot* — so our **adapter
  has a Ø31.5 h7 spigot protruding 4–5 mm**); Ø6 H7 hole for the
  robot-side dowel; 4 × Ø6.6 (M6 clearance) through-holes on Ø50; 0.04
  mm flatness over the Ø63 face. Bolt counter-bore for M6 SHCS head
  (Ø11 × 6.4 mm).
- 80-6-M8 variant: Ø100 face OD; Ø50 h7 spigot; Ø8 H7 dowel hole; 6 ×
  Ø9 (M8 clearance) through-holes on Ø80; 0.04 mm flatness over Ø100.
  Bolt counter-bore for M8 SHCS head (Ø13 × 8 mm).

Bottom face (gripper side, both variants):

- Mate to the **enclosure bottom M4 flange** described in
  `../ROV_INTEGRATION.md §1`: 4 × M4 through-holes on a 40–50 mm bolt
  circle (matching the gripper flange BCD, to be confirmed in
  `gripper.py`), with PTFE / nylon isolation bushings per
  `../ROV_INTEGRATION.md §1b`. Centerbore Ø10.5 mm to clear the D-coupler
  exit (Ø10 D-coupler + 0.5 mm radial clearance).
- The bottom face is a printed PETG-on-PETG joint with 1.5–1.8 N·m M4
  torque (same fastener spec as `../ROV_INTEGRATION.md §1c`).

Cable channel (both variants):

- Side-exit: an 8 mm × 4 mm rectangular slot through the adapter wall at
  90° to the dowel-pin axis (i.e., at 9 o'clock on the +Xm clock face)
  to carry the RS-485 + 12 V cable from the actuator out the side. The
  channel does **not** pass through the bolt circle; it goes through the
  adapter wall between d2 (Ø63) and the gripper-side flange OD. A
  rubber grommet or M8 cable-gland adapter (Ø7.5–9 cable OD) seats in
  the slot.
- Adapter height (puck thickness): **8–12 mm** for the 50-4-M6 variant;
  **12–16 mm** for the 80-6-M8 variant.

**Bolt-length / adapter-thickness pairing (critical — UR protrusion
ceiling).** The screw threads (not the head) are what penetrate the
cobot face; the UR ceiling is **10 mm of thread into the robot**. The
bolt goes head-down from the top (cobot) side of the adapter — head
recessed in the adapter's counter-bore — and the shank threads into
the robot's blind M6 hole. The geometry relation is simply:

> `thread_into_robot = screw_length − adapter_thickness`

(The counter-bore depth only controls whether the M6 SHCS head sits
fully recessed — set `counter_bore_depth ≥ head_height = 6.0 mm`.)

We also want at least **6 mm of thread engagement** into the robot
(~1×D for M6, which is the conventional minimum for full preload in
steel-into-steel; this is conservative since the UR receiving thread is
into the wrist housing, also steel). So the safe window is
`6 mm ≤ (screw_length − adapter_thickness) ≤ 10 mm`.

| Adapter thickness | M6 SHCS length | Thread into robot | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| 8 mm | M6 × 12 | 4 mm | **marginal** — below 6 mm minimum engagement |
| 8 mm | M6 × 14 | 6 mm | **safe** — at minimum engagement |
| 8 mm | M6 × 16 | 8 mm | **safe** — recommended |
| 8 mm | M6 × 20 | 12 mm | **unsafe** — exceeds UR ceiling |
| 10 mm | M6 × 16 | 6 mm | **safe** — at minimum engagement |
| 10 mm | M6 × 18 | 8 mm | **safe** — recommended |
| 10 mm | M6 × 20 | 10 mm | **at limit** — equals UR ceiling exactly |
| 12 mm | M6 × 18 | 6 mm | **safe** |
| 12 mm | M6 × 20 | 8 mm | **safe** — recommended |
| 12 mm | M6 × 25 | 13 mm | **unsafe** |

**Recommended primary pair: 10 mm adapter + M6×18 SHCS** (8 mm thread
into robot — within 6–10 mm window, 2 mm headroom under UR ceiling). For
the 80-6-M8 variant on UR20, equivalent reasoning gives **14 mm adapter
+ M8×20 SHCS** (6 mm thread into robot — at M8 minimum engagement, 4 mm
under UR20 ceiling). Note: M8 minimum engagement is 8 mm (1×D); revisit
when UR20 thread depth is confirmed.

### 10.3 Material recommendation

| Material | Yield (MPa) | E (GPa) | Mass (50-4-M6) | When to use | When NOT to use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **PA12-GF 30% (printed, e.g. MJF Nylon-12 PA Glass-Filled)** | 50 (printed) `[19]` | 3.0 | ~35–45 g | Default — sufficient stiffness, dimensional stability of Ø31.5 h7 spigot under cobot face flatness 0.04 mm, holds M6 thread engagement reliably | High-vibration or > 10 kg payload arm; printed surfaces will not reliably hold 0.04 mm flatness without post-machining |
| **Aluminium 6082-T6 (machined)** | 250 | 70 | ~100 g | UR20 / UR16e service, KUKA LBR iiwa, ABB GoFa-12, or any moment > 5 N·m | Subsea (galvanic with stainless bolts in seawater) — but this adapter is **dry-only**, so the galvanic constraint doesn't apply |
| **PETG (printed, 100% walls)** | 25 | 1.5 | ~30 g | Demo / pre-mating dry fit only | Anything load-bearing — PETG creeps under preload |
| **PEEK (machined)** | 100 | 3.8 | ~50 g | Aerospace / lab high-spec | Cost — likely $200+ per adapter |

**Default selection for first article: PA12-GF on MJF (HP MultiJet
Fusion) or an SLS process.** Reasons:

1. PA12-GF is dimensionally stable enough for Ø31.5 h7 spigot directly
   off the printer (within ±0.05 mm typically — `[19]`), so no post-
   machining is needed for fit; only the top flat face may need a
   0.5 mm PTFE shim to take up the 0.04 mm flatness spec.
2. PA12-GF holds M6 through-holes without cracking the polymer at the
   recommended 9 N·m UR torque (verified by external sources — needs
   our own pull-test in `../BENCH_TEST.md`).
3. The bottom-face fastener pattern (4 × M4 through-holes with
   isolation bushings) is identical to our existing flange, so the
   adapter is a *printable PETG part* if we prototype, *PA12-GF* for
   production demo.

**For UR20 and KUKA LBR iiwa 14 R820 + 80-6-M8**: switch to **machined
aluminium 6082-T6**. The 80-6-M8 face carries a 16 N·m bolt torque per
M8 8.8 fastener (UR20 spec `[6]`) and the print-stress-concentration at
M8 counter-bores becomes the failure mode. Estimated bench-rig cost: a
small machine shop quotes Al 6082 50-4-M6 adapter at ~AUD 80–120, 80-6-M8
at ~AUD 150–200 (estimate, single-piece).

| Surface | Spec | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Top face (mates to cobot) | 0.04 mm flatness, Ra ≤ 1.6 μm `[1 Fig.1]` | Either machined post-print, or use a 0.5 mm PTFE shim (commercial PTFE washer Ø63 × Ø31.5 × 0.5 mm) — PTFE conformal-deforms under M6 9 N·m bolts and absorbs print waviness |

### 10.5 Important constraint — this adapter is **dry-only**

The XW540 actuator's polymer canister (described in `../SELECTION.md
§"T2 sealing"`) is **not rated for the cobot wrist's IP rating** and the
cobot itself is not rated for wet operation (UR e-Series is IP54, Franka
FR3 IP40, ABB GoFa CRB 15000 IP54 `[7][11]`). The ISO 9409-1 adapter
described here is **for dry bench testing, demos, and lab use only.**
It is not the underwater interface. Underwater the gripper continues to
use its bottom M4 flange to a sealed ROV penetrator (per
`../ROV_INTEGRATION.md`). Mark this on the printed part with a stamped
"DRY USE ONLY" annotation per `[1 §6]` marking guidance.

## 11. Open questions / things we couldn't verify

Items we'd like to verify before committing the CAD:

1. **Exact thread depth t4 on the UR e-Series 50-4-M6 face.** UR caps
   bolt-protrusion at 10 mm, but the underlying threaded depth into the
   robot is implied at "at least 10 mm" — not publicly published. We
   should bench-measure on a UR5e at the partner lab. **Risk:** if the
   robot blind-thread is shorter than 10 mm we need to use M6×8 or M6×10
   length screws; M6×12 is unsafe (would bottom out and possibly short).
2. **Franka FR3 / Panda exact dowel pin diameter and projection.** The
   "DIN ISO 9409-1-A50" designation says A50 (50 mm pitch, form A) —
   pin is per `[1]` Ø6 H7 — but Franka manuals don't explicitly give
   the projection length. Need a measurement.
3. **Kinova Gen3 native interface to ISO adapter:** Kinova ships a
   "Reference Design Package" with both mechanical (4×M5 → 50-4-M6) and
   electrical (24 V/0.5 A + 5 V/2 A + Ethernet) adapters. We have only
   the user-guide screenshot, not the CAD. Need the package from
   Kinova (free download, requires registration).
4. **Fanuc CRX-10iA exact flange dimensions.** Fanuc CRX series uses
   `50-4-M6` per common knowledge but the Fanuc EE manual is not
   publicly mirrored on the open web. Need a partner-lab measurement or
   a paid Fanuc datasheet.
5. **Mecademic Meca500 exact bolt-circle and locating-pin position.**
   Mecademic publishes the screw spec (4×M3, 1.5 N·m, optional Ø3 pin)
   but not the bolt-circle BCD. CAD STEP file `[14]` would resolve.
6. **0.04 mm flatness achievable in PA12-GF off the printer.** We've
   used PA12 elsewhere in the project but our flatness measurements
   are for the printed gripper bottom face, not for a SLS/MJF sample.
   Need a first-article inspection on a Ø63 PA12-GF coupon.
7. **UR Tool I/O M8 0/12/24 V switching latency.** When we change
   set_tool_voltage() does the current XW540 stay within its absolute-
   maximum supply transient? Worth checking the URScript spec.
8. **Whether KUKA's 7×M6 face is compatible with a 4-bolt adapter
   without backing out the unused KUKA-side bolts**. From `[12]` the
   media flange has 7 threaded holes — adapter would use 4 of them
   (the 0°, 90°, 180°, 270° positions, which are 4 of the 7) and leave
   3 unbolted. This is mechanically *safe* (4 × M6 8.8 at 9 N·m gives
   ample preload) but may not be vendor-approved.
