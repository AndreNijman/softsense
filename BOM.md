# Bill of Materials — Underwater geared four-bar / Fin Ray gripper

Production BOM for the gripper defined in `gripper.py` (geared four-bar, single
input shaft, compliant TPU Fin Ray fingers, **flooded** housing for underwater
use). This is the *what-to-buy / what-to-print* list. See `PRINTING.md` for how
to orient and slice each printed part, `UNDERWATER.md` for material chemistry in
seawater, and `ASSEMBLY.md` for how it goes together.

Headline build target: **flooded, all metal parts 316/A4 stainless, rigid
polymer structure, ether-based TPU fingers.** There is nothing to seal.

the enclosure opening, see PRINTING.md). Pivot pins shown in the model are
representations of real hardware (Section 2), not prints.

| `drive_arm_L` (gear sector + crank arm + **integral input shaft** + rear D-coupler) | 1 | **Acetal / glass-filled nylon**; for real torque add a **316 SS shaft insert** (bore the hub for a Ø8 mm 316 rod, bonded/pinned) | This part carries all the drive torque through its integral shaft. Ether-based TPU resists hydrolysis in sustained/warm immersion (ester-based crumbles); 95A balances conformance vs. grip force. \* Rough single-piece estimates at the recommended walls/infill in PRINTING.md;
calibrate against your slicer and material. **Total filament: roughly
~200–280 g rigid + ~50–70 g TPU.**

**Material rationale (seawater):** rigid structure in glass-filled nylon or
acetal because both have very low water uptake, do not hydrolyze (unlike PLA),
and creep little under sustained hydrostatic load; acetal additionally
self-lubricates where the gear teeth and bushing run wet. Fingers in
ether-based TPU because the compliant grip depends on flex and ester-based TPU
hydrolyzes underwater. PETG/ASA are acceptable for shallow, short, or freshwater
work but bias to glass-nylon/acetal for deep or long dives.

## 2. Hardware (fasteners) — A4/316 stainless ONLY

Every metal part is **A4 (316) stainless**. This is deliberate: in seawater,
dissimilar metals in contact form a galvanic cell and the less-noble metal
corrodes preferentially. Keeping **every metal part in one stainless family
(all 316/A4)** means there is **no internal galvanic pair** to drive corrosion.
316 (A4) is chosen over 304 (A2) for its molybdenum content, which resists
chloride pitting in salt water. Do **not** substitute zinc-plated steel, brass,
or carbon steel — they rust/pit fast and create galvanic couples.

### 2.1 Pivot pins + locknuts

The four-bar has **7 pivot joints** that become real hardware. Count: **1 drive
axle (A_R)** + **2 follower axles (B_R, B_L)** + **4 finger pins
(C_R, D_R, C_L, D_L)**. The **LEFT drive arm's axle is its own integral printed
shaft running in a plain bushing** (Section 2.4) — so there is **no A_L pin**.

Pin bores in the model are ~Ø4.6 mm shafts in ~Ø4.9–5.1 mm clearance bores
(`PIN_R = 2.3`, `PRINT_CLEAR = 0.25`). Spec to match: **M4** socket-head screw +
nylon-insert locknut (or Ø4.6 mm 316 dowel pin + E-clips). M3 is the smaller
acceptable option if you ream bores down.

| Item | Size | Material | Qty | Locates |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Drive-arm axle | M4 socket-head cap screw (or Ø4.6 mm dowel) | A4/316 SS | 1 | A_R (right crank/gear pivot) — seats in the back-wall boss |
| Follower axles | M4 socket-head cap screw (or Ø4.6 mm dowel) | A4/316 SS | 2 | B_R, B_L (follower pivots) — seat in back-wall bosses |
| Finger pins | M4 socket-head cap screw (or Ø4.6 mm dowel), ~24 mm spanning the stack | A4/316 SS | 4 | C_R, D_R, C_L, D_L (couple finger to coupler CD) |
| **Nylon-insert locknuts** | M4 (nyloc) | A4/316 SS | **7** | one per pivot screw — retains the pin and sets pivot freedom |

> If you use **dowel pins + E-clips** instead of screw+nyloc, swap the 7 locknuts
> for 7 E-clip pairs (and matching grooved 316 dowels). Either approach is fine;
> the screw+nyloc route is the easiest to set "snug-but-free."

### 2.4 Input-shaft plain bushing (NOT a sealed bearing)

| Item | Size | Material | Qty | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Plain bushing / sleeve | bore to clear the Ø8 mm shaft; OD to fit the Ø10 mm back-wall bore (`SHAFT_BORE_R = 5.0`, shaft `SHAFT_R = 4.0`) | **PTFE or acetal** | 1 | Presses into the back-wall shaft bore; the integral input shaft turns inside it, wet. |

**Why a plain bushing and no sealed bearing for flooded use:** in a flooded
design there is **no pressure differential and no dry cavity to protect**, so
there is nothing for a seal/bearing to keep out. A sealed ball bearing is
actively worse here — it **traps grit and seawater inside the race** where it
cannot flush, then corrodes and seizes. A PTFE/acetal **plain bushing runs wet,
self-lubricates, needs no grease, and flushes clean** with a freshwater rinse.
**Avoid oil-impregnated bronze (Oilite):** its impregnated oil leaches out when
flooded, leaving a dry porous bushing that wears fast.

## 4. Why this is corrosion-proof underwater

The gripper is **flooded and single-metal**. It runs wet by design — water fills
the housing through the drain/flood holes, so internal and external pressure
equalize and the thin printed wall never sees a pressure differential (nothing
to crush or implode, depth-independent). With nothing sealed, there are no seals
to fail. Every metal part is **A4/316 stainless**, so the assembly has **no
internal galvanic pair**: 316's molybdenum resists chloride pitting, and keeping
one stainless family means no less-noble metal is driven to corrode. The rigid
structure is **glass-filled nylon or acetal** (low water uptake, no hydrolysis —
unlike PLA), and the fingers are **ether-based TPU** (does not hydrolyze in
sustained immersion). The input shaft rides in a **PTFE/acetal plain bushing**
that runs wet and flushes clean — no sealed bearing to trap grit, no Oilite to
leach oil. Optional **nylon/PTFE isolation washers** break the galvanic path to a
dissimilar-metal robot arm. Net: nothing to rust, nothing to seal, nothing to
crush — rinse with fresh water after each salt dive and it stays reliable.
